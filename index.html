2010: The Work Begins

In 2010, the U.S. Forest Service Monongahela National Forest (MNF) began a partnership with Green Forests Work (GFW), a nonprofit housed in The University of Kentucky's Forestry Department, and together they began conducting a suite of restoration activities on formerly strip mined lands, including non-native species removal, soil decompaction, organic matter loading, wetland creation, and reforestation. In the short term, the goal is to create an early successional habitat, with the ultimate goal being to establish a red spruce dominant forest. Ancillary benefits include improved water quality, enhanced wildlife habitat, and improved ecosystem services, such as carbon sequestration.

This embedded map was created to showcase the mined land restoration work completed from 2010 through the end of 2024, and to layout some of the plans for future work.

Lambert South, the area I started working on in 2014, and a view of the mine sediment ponds below, which are now being decommissioned into wetland complexes as part of the newest phase of restoration work. Photo by Matt Barton.

As the restoration work changes gears to decommissioning mine sediment ponds, local tourism and recreation, especially mountain biking and camping, brings people into the area that may be curious about the work that has been done over the years. The restoration work is messy-looking, with exotic trees uprooted and thrown about, but the planted trees are growing and transforming the sites ever so slowly.

The most unique thing about this work is that it was done in a way that, driving deeper into the site, you can see the chronological succession of the restoration work going from the oldest to youngest. The oldest sites are nearest the main road, and the youngest sites are the furthest away and have somewhat spread beyond the main Mower Tract area.

In 2022, red spruce planted in 2011 at our very first restoration area, Barton Bench, stand taller than GFW's founder and President, Chris Barton. Photo by Anna Branduzzi.

In 2022, MNF and GFW staff check on one of the first constructed wetlands from 2011 on Barton Bench. Photo by Anna Branduzzi.

U.S. Forest Service and Green Forests Work partners are frequently going back into "completed" restoration areas from previous years to do improvements, such as invasive species treatments, seeding of native plants and flowers for pollinator habitat, supplemental tree plantings, wetland plantings, volunteer events, and etc. The goal is for this map to be the place to view all past work done in each restoration area, and to document all new enhancement work that occurs.

To date, nearly 900,000 seedling have been planted on almost 1,500 acres at the Mower Tract on Cheat Mountain in the MNF.
